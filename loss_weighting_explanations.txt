🧠 1. Inverse Frequency (the core idea)
🔹 Problem

Your dataset is heavily imbalanced:

car = 1,021,811
train = 179

Without fixing this, your model learns:

“If I just predict car a lot, I’ll be mostly correct.”

🔹 Solution: inverse frequency

We assign weight:
	​w_c = 1 / count_c

So:
    Big classes → small weight
    Small classes → big weight

🔍 Example with your data

Let’s compare two classes:

car:
    w_car = 1 / 1021811 ≈ 0.000000978

train:
    w_train = 1 / 170 ≈ 0.00559

👉 Train gets ~5700× more importance than car.

⚖️ 2. Normalization (make numbers usable)

Right now weights are tiny decimals — not ideal.

So we rescale them:

weights = weights / weights.min()

This makes the smallest weight = 1.

🔍 What happens?
car (smallest weight) → becomes 1
everything else scales relative to it

So now:

Class	Relative Weight
car	1
bus	~60
bike	~100
motor	~238
train	~5700 🚨
⚠️ Problem

That 5700 is dangerous:

gradients explode
training unstable
model may overfit to rare noise

🧯 3. Softening the weights (VERY IMPORTANT)

This is where you control the aggression of weighting.

Instead of:

    𝑤 = 1 / count

We “soften” it.

✅ Option A: Square root (BEST START)

    w = 1 / sqrt(count)

🔍 Why it works

Square root reduces extremes:

| Class | Count     | sqrt(count) | weight |
| ----- | --------- | ----------- | ------ |
| car   | 1,021,811 | ~1010       | small  |
| train | 179       | ~13         | bigger |


Now ratio is:

	1010 / 13​ ≈ 77

👉 Instead of 5700×, now only ~77×

💡 Interpretation
Still favors rare classes ✅
Doesn’t go crazy ❌

✅ Option B: Log scaling (even softer)

    𝑤 = 1 / log(count + 1)


This compresses even more.

🧠 4. Big picture intuition

Think of weighting like a volume knob 🎚️

| Method            | Effect                     |
| ----------------- | -------------------------- |
| No weights        | model ignores rare classes |
| Inverse (1/count) | VERY aggressive            |
| sqrt(1/count)     | balanced ✅                 |
| log(1/count)      | very gentle                |


🎯 Final mental model
    Inverse frequency → fairness
    Normalization → stability
    Softening → realism
